## 1. Causality-inspired Federated Learning for Dynamic Spatio-Temporal Graphs
**Score:** 8/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29384v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29384v1)

**Problem:** Federated graph learning methods assume uniform feature transferability across clients, causing spurious representation entanglement and negative transfer when applied to heterogeneous dynamic spatio-temporal graphs.

**Solution:** SC-FSGL uses a Conditional Separation Module with client-conditioned soft interventions to disentangle invariant causal spatio-temporal factors from client-specific noise, and a Causal Codebook for cross-client prototype alignment via contrastive learning.

**Limitations:** The 'causality' is operationalized as invariance under interventions rather than through formal causal identification — no SCM or do-calculus grounding is provided. The disentanglement relies on contrastive learning heuristics without theoretical guarantees of causal identifiability.

**Relevance to your work:** Directly addresses both Causality and Forecasting keywords. The representation-level intervention approach echoes the causal representation learning methodology in the user's past work on causal representations. The problem of disentangling invariant causal features from spurious correlations under distribution shift is central to the user's research agenda.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The Conditional Separation Module achieves soft interventions via masking, but formal causal identification of what constitutes a 'causal' vs. 'spurious' spatio-temporal feature remains open. Grounding this in the user's causal representation framework could provide identifiability guarantees currently absent. Connecting federated heterogeneity to instrumental variable approaches — where client identity acts as an instrument — could formalize when cross-client transfer is causally valid.

## 2. Central limit theorems for the outputs of fully convolutional neural networks with time series input
**Score:** 6/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29612v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29612v1)

**Problem:** Despite widespread use of deep learning for time series classification and forecasting, there is almost no theoretical characterization of the statistical behavior of network outputs under dependent input processes.

**Solution:** The paper proves asymptotic Gaussianity of FCN-GAP outputs when inputs are short-range dependent linear processes, leveraging time series theory tools, and proposes a generalized global weighted pooling with learnable slowly-varying coefficients.

**Limitations:** Results are restricted to short-range dependent linear processes and FCN architectures with GAP — long-range dependence (common in financial or climate time series) and recurrent/attention architectures are excluded. The practical utility of the learnable weighted pooling extension is not empirically validated.

**Relevance to your work:** Relevant to the Forecasting keyword via theoretical foundations for DL on time series. The statistical framing aligns with the user's quantitative background in representation learning and causal analysis. However, the paper does not address causality and is a pure theory contribution.

**Research ideas this opens up:** Extending these CLT results to time series with causal structure — where inputs come from a linear structural causal model rather than a generic linear process — could formalize when a neural forecaster's outputs are causally interpretable. The Gaussian limit could enable uncertainty quantification for causal effect estimates obtained via neural network predictors, connecting directly to the user's work on sparse causal effect estimation.

## 3. One-for-All: A Lightweight Stabilized and Parameter-Efficient Pre-trained LLM for Time Series Forecasting
**Score:** 6/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29756v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29756v1)

**Problem:** Adapting LLMs for time series forecasting is hindered by prohibitive memory and compute requirements, while existing PEFT methods lack gradient stability guarantees at low ranks.

**Solution:** rsLoRA introduces Gaussian rank-stabilization into low-rank adapters with provable gradient stability, injecting trainable matrices only into positional embeddings and output layers while freezing attention weights — reducing parameters by 6.8–21× versus SOTA.

**Limitations:** Evaluated only on standard benchmarks (ETT, Weather, M3, M4); the claim that frozen self-attention is sufficient is not ablated against fine-tuned attention. The rank-stabilization guarantees apply to gradient norms but not to downstream forecasting quality.

**Relevance to your work:** Directly relevant to the Forecasting keyword with a novel methodological contribution (rsLoRA). The gradient stability framing has some formal rigor aligned with the user's quantitative background, though no causality connection exists.

**Research ideas this opens up:** Applying rsLoRA-style parameter-efficient adaptation to LLMs for causal inference tasks — e.g., adapting a pre-trained LLM to perform causal effect estimation from observational text data — is an unexplored direction. The rank-stabilization mechanism could also inform theoretical analysis of when low-rank approximations preserve causal structure in representation learning.

## 4. Improving Ensemble Forecasts of Abnormally Deflecting Tropical Cyclones with Fused Atmosphere-Ocean-Terrain Data
**Score:** 5/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29200v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29200v1)

**Problem:** Existing deep learning TC forecasting models handle only homogeneous trajectory data and fail on abnormally deflecting cyclones due to complex atmosphere-ocean-terrain interactions.

**Solution:** The paper introduces AOT-TCs, the first multimodal dataset fusing atmosphere, ocean, and terrain variables, paired with an explicit cross-domain coupling architecture for TC track forecasting.

**Limitations:** Focused exclusively on the Northwest Pacific basin (2017–2024); the degree to which the coupling architecture captures true physical interactions versus learned correlations is unclear. No ablation of individual data modalities' contributions.

**Relevance to your work:** Directly relevant to the Forecasting keyword. However, the contribution is primarily domain-specific dataset construction and application engineering rather than novel forecasting methodology. Limited connection to the user's causality work.

**Research ideas this opens up:** Abnormal deflection events could be modeled as causal distribution shifts — where the standard action-trajectory causal mechanism breaks down. Applying causal change point detection (directly in the user's past work) to identify when TC dynamics switch regimes could give both scientific insight and forecasting improvement. The multimodal fusion raises questions about identifying which data sources are causally sufficient versus spuriously correlated for trajectory prediction.

## 5. Hybrid Quantum-Classical Spatiotemporal Forecasting for 3D Cloud Fields
**Score:** 4/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29407v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29407v1)

**Problem:** 3D cloud field forecasting suffers from locality-biased representations that fail to capture nonlocal cross-layer dependencies and multiscale dynamics.

**Solution:** QENO combines a classical spatiotemporal encoder with a topology-aware quantum enhancement block that models nonlocal couplings in latent space, integrated via a dynamic fusion temporal unit.

**Limitations:** The 'quantum' component is quantum-inspired classical simulation, not actual quantum computation — the claimed quantum advantage is not substantiated on quantum hardware. The nonlocal coupling rationale could likely be achieved with standard attention mechanisms at lower complexity.

**Relevance to your work:** Relevant to the Forecasting keyword through spatiotemporal prediction, but the quantum angle is peripheral to the user's statistical/causal ML background. The methodology is not connected to causal inference or representation learning.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The topology-aware graph structures used for 'nonlocal couplings' are essentially learned adjacency structures — making these causally interpretable (i.e., identifying which spatial couplings are causal vs. correlational) would be a novel direction. This connects to the user's causal change point detection work: can we detect when the causal topology of a 3D field changes over time?
