## 1. Causality-inspired Federated Learning for Dynamic Spatio-Temporal Graphs
**Score:** 7/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29384v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29384v1)

**Problem:** Federated graph learning on dynamic spatio-temporal graphs suffers from spurious representation entanglement and negative transfer because existing methods treat all features as equally transferable across heterogeneous clients.

**Solution:** SC-FSGL uses a Conditional Separation Module with client-conditioned soft interventions to disentangle invariant causal spatio-temporal factors from spurious signals, and a Causal Codebook with contrastive learning for cross-client causal prototype alignment.

**Limitations:** The method's notion of causality is operationalized as invariance across clients via intervention simulation — this is a representation learning heuristic rather than a principled causal identification argument, and there are no guarantees that disentangled representations correspond to true causal factors. Evaluation is limited to traffic/mobility STG datasets.

**Relevance to your work:** This paper directly combines causality and spatio-temporal forecasting — both core keywords for the user. The use of interventions and causal disentanglement for robust federated learning is methodologically adjacent to causal representation learning. The spurious correlation problem under distribution shift is central to causal inference research.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The soft intervention via client-conditioned masks is a practical approximation — but what does it identify from a formal causal perspective? Connecting this to the potential outcomes framework or structural causal models could yield identifiability results that bound when this heuristic succeeds or fails. The causal codebook idea also raises a question about whether causal prototypes in spatio-temporal graphs can be discovered without labeled intervention data, linking to the user's work on causal representations in observational settings with unmeasured confounding.

## 2. Central limit theorems for the outputs of fully convolutional neural networks with time series input
**Score:** 6/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29612v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29612v1)

**Problem:** Despite widespread use of deep learning for time series forecasting and classification, almost no theoretical guarantees exist for neural network behavior when inputs are temporally dependent (short-range dependent linear processes).

**Solution:** The authors prove that outputs of fully convolutional networks with global average pooling are asymptotically Gaussian under short-range dependence, using tools from time series theory, and propose a learnable global weighted pooling generalization.

**Limitations:** Results are restricted to short-range dependent linear processes; long-memory processes (common in financial or climate data) are not covered. The asymptotic framework applies as series length grows, which may not reflect finite-sample behavior in practice.

**Relevance to your work:** This is directly relevant to the user's forecasting interest and touches on theoretical foundations for deep learning with time series — an underexplored area. While not directly about causality, understanding when neural network outputs are asymptotically normal has implications for causal hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification in forecasting pipelines.

**Research ideas this opens up:** If FCN outputs are asymptotically Gaussian under temporal dependence, this could enable principled confidence intervals for neural forecasters — a key gap in operationalizing causal forecasting. Crucially: do these CLT results hold when the input process undergoes a causal change point (as in the user's prior work), and if so, can this be used to build distribution-free tests for detecting model breakdown at change points?

## 3. Improving Ensemble Forecasts of Abnormally Deflecting Tropical Cyclones with Fused Atmosphere-Ocean-Terrain Data
**Score:** 5/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29200v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29200v1)

**Problem:** Deep learning TC track forecasting methods handle only single-type sequential data or homogeneous meteorological variables and fail systematically on abnormally deflecting tropical cyclones, which are disproportionately dangerous.

**Solution:** The paper constructs AOT-TCs, the first multimodal dataset integrating atmospheric, oceanic, and terrain variables, and proposes a TC forecasting model with an explicit atmosphere-ocean-terrain coupling architecture.

**Limitations:** The dataset and model are limited to the Northwest Pacific basin (2017-2024), and it is unclear how the coupling architecture would generalize to other basins with different terrain profiles. The explicit coupling mechanism is not deeply ablated against simpler fusion baselines.

**Relevance to your work:** This directly addresses forecasting with strong empirical methodology (8 years of real TC cases). The multimodal fusion raises an implicit causal identification problem — distinguishing causal drivers from correlated covariates across domains — loosely connecting to the user's causal analysis work.

**Research ideas this opens up:** Abnormal TC deflection is precisely a regime-change event — causal change-point detection methods (as in the user's prior work on causal change point detection and localization) could be applied to identify when and why TC tracks diverge from typical dynamics. A causal lens on which atmosphere-ocean-terrain interactions actually drive deflection versus merely correlate with it could yield more robust models under climate non-stationarity.

## 4. One-for-All: A Lightweight Stabilized and Parameter-Efficient Pre-trained LLM for Time Series Forecasting
**Score:** 5/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29756v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29756v1)

**Problem:** Deploying pre-trained LLMs for multivariate time series forecasting is hindered by prohibitive computational and memory costs, and existing PEFT methods (like LoRA) suffer from gradient instability at low ranks.

**Solution:** rsLoRA introduces a mathematically grounded rank-stabilization mechanism (Gaussian rank-stabilized adapters) that enables provably stable gradients at low ranks, achieving 6.8-21x parameter reduction and 168-1776x memory reduction versus SOTA baselines.

**Limitations:** The rank-stabilization theory assumes certain distributional properties of initialization that may not hold after domain-specific fine-tuning. Evaluation is limited to standard time series benchmarks; performance on distribution-shifted or causally heterogeneous datasets is untested.

**Relevance to your work:** Directly relevant to the user's forecasting interest. The parameter-efficient fine-tuning of LLMs for time series is practically important. However, the contribution is primarily architectural/optimization rather than causal or methodological in the statistical sense.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The rank-stabilization insight raises a question for causal representation learning: do low-rank structural equation models or sparse causal graphs have analogous stability conditions during learning? Testing whether rsLoRA-adapted LLMs maintain calibrated uncertainty under distribution shift (e.g., regime changes from causal change points) would bridge this work with causal forecasting concerns.

## 5. Meteorology-Driven GPT4AP: A Multi-Task Forecasting LLM for Atmospheric Air Pollution in Data-Scarce Settings
**Score:** 4/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29974v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29974v1)

**Problem:** Air pollution forecasting in data-scarce regions suffers from poor generalization because most models are trained on dense observation networks and do not transfer to sparse settings or cross-station scenarios.

**Solution:** GPT4AP fine-tunes a frozen GPT-2 backbone using rsLoRA on meteorological and air quality data, enabling few-shot and zero-shot cross-station forecasting via parameter-efficient multi-task adaptation.

**Limitations:** The GPT-2 backbone was not pre-trained on meteorological data, so performance gains may partly reflect rsLoRA regularization rather than genuine language model transfer. Zero-shot results are evaluated on stations within the same geographic region, limiting out-of-distribution generalization claims.

**Relevance to your work:** Relevant to the user's forecasting interest but is a domain application paper using parameter-efficient LLM fine-tuning. The causal structure of the atmosphere-pollution relationship is not modeled — the paper treats it as a black-box sequence prediction task.

**Research ideas this opens up:** Zero-shot cross-station transfer implicitly requires the model to identify invariant causal features across locations — this is precisely the invariant risk minimization / causal invariance problem. Explicitly incorporating causal structure (which meteorological variables causally drive pollution vs. merely correlate) could improve zero-shot generalization in a principled way, directly connecting to the user's expertise in causal effect estimation under unmeasured confounding.
