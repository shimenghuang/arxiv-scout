## 1. Causality-inspired Federated Learning for Dynamic Spatio-Temporal Graphs
**Score:** 8/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29384v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29384v1)

**Problem:** Federated learning over dynamic spatio-temporal graphs suffers from spurious representation entanglement and negative transfer because existing methods assume all features are equally transferable across clients, ignoring heterogeneity and client-specific noise.

**Solution:** SC-FSGL explicitly disentangles causal (invariant spatio-temporal) features from spurious signals via a Conditional Separation Module with soft interventions, and aligns causal prototypes across clients using a Causal Codebook with contrastive learning.

**Limitations:** The 'causal' claims rely on representation disentanglement via masked interventions without formal identifiability guarantees — it is unclear what causal model is being implicitly assumed. The evaluation uses five STG datasets but all are traffic/mobility domains, limiting breadth.

**Relevance to your work:** This paper directly engages both the user's Causality and Forecasting keywords. The use of causal disentanglement for invariant learning in spatio-temporal settings connects to the user's interest in causal representation learning and causal change point detection. The federated setting adds a novel angle — causal structures across heterogeneous data sources.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The SC-FSGL framework implicitly assumes that causal features are invariant across clients, but in spatio-temporal settings the causal structure itself may change (e.g., traffic patterns shift post-construction). Combining this with the user's causal change point detection work could yield a federated framework that jointly detects structural changes and adapts causal representations across clients. Additionally, the Conditional Separation Module's soft interventions could be grounded in a formal structural causal model, enabling identifiability analysis analogous to the user's work on unmeasured confounding.

## 2. Improving Ensemble Forecasts of Abnormally Deflecting Tropical Cyclones with Fused Atmosphere-Ocean-Terrain Data
**Score:** 7/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29200v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29200v1)

**Problem:** Deep learning models for tropical cyclone track forecasting can only handle homogeneous meteorological inputs and fail on abnormally deflecting TCs, which exhibit complex multi-domain physical interactions not captured by single-modality approaches.

**Solution:** The authors construct AOT-TCs, the first multimodal dataset integrating atmospheric, oceanic, and terrain variables for TC forecasting in the Northwest Pacific, and propose a model with explicit atmosphere-ocean-terrain coupling architecture evaluated on 2017-2024 TC cases.

**Limitations:** The explicit coupling architecture is tailored to TC physics; it is unclear whether the multimodal fusion strategy generalizes to other forecasting domains. The paper focuses on the Northwest Pacific, limiting assessment of geographic generalization.

**Relevance to your work:** This paper directly targets the user's Forecasting keyword with a rigorous empirical study across 8 years of real TC cases. The multimodal data fusion raises questions about causal identification — when multiple physical domains are fused, distinguishing causal drivers from correlated covariates is non-trivial, linking loosely to the user's causal analysis research.

**Research ideas this opens up:** Multimodal physical forecasting raises a sharp causal identification problem: which of atmosphere, ocean, or terrain variables causally drive deflection versus merely correlate with it? Applying causal discovery or interventional analysis (e.g., the user's sparse causal effect estimation methods) to disentangle drivers from confounders in the AOT-TCs dataset could significantly improve model interpretability. Additionally, the rare-event nature of abnormal deflections suggests a causal change point detection framing — the user's prior work on causal change point detection is directly applicable here.

## 3. Central limit theorems for the outputs of fully convolutional neural networks with time series input
**Score:** 6/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29612v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29612v1)

**Problem:** Despite widespread use of CNNs for time series classification and forecasting, there is almost no statistical theory characterizing the distributional behavior of their outputs when inputs are temporally dependent.

**Solution:** The authors prove that FCN outputs with global average pooling are asymptotically Gaussian for short-range dependent linear process inputs, and propose a generalized global weighted pooling layer with slowly varying learnable coefficients grounded in this theory.

**Limitations:** Results are restricted to short-range dependent linear processes — long-memory processes (common in many forecasting applications) are not covered. The practical implications for finite-sample forecasting tasks are not empirically demonstrated.

**Relevance to your work:** This paper provides theoretical grounding for CNN-based time series models, adjacent to the user's Forecasting interest. The theory-first approach aligns with the user's methodologically rigorous background (sparse causal effect estimation, compositional data methods), though the paper itself does not address causality.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The CLT result for FCN outputs opens the door to valid uncertainty quantification for CNN-based forecasters. More importantly, if one could prove analogous CLTs for causal estimators implemented via neural networks (e.g., neural IV estimators), it would provide the first rigorous asymptotic theory for neural causal effect estimation, directly relevant to the user's Mendelian randomization and instrumental variable work. The proposed learnable pooling layer could also be adapted to weight time points by estimated causal influence.

## 4. One-for-All: A Lightweight Stabilized and Parameter-Efficient Pre-trained LLM for Time Series Forecasting
**Score:** 6/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29756v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29756v1)

**Problem:** Adapting pre-trained LLMs for multivariate time series forecasting is hindered by prohibitive computational and memory costs, while existing PEFT methods like LoRA lack provable gradient stability at low ranks.

**Solution:** One-for-All introduces Gaussian Rank-Stabilized LoRA (rsLoRA) with a mathematically grounded rank-stabilization mechanism injected into positional embeddings and output layers only, achieving 6.8-21x fewer trainable parameters and 168-1776x smaller memory footprint versus SOTA.

**Limitations:** The theoretical rank-stabilization guarantee applies to gradient norms but does not directly bound forecasting error or generalization. Evaluation covers only standard benchmarks (ETT, Weather, M3, M4); real-world distribution shift scenarios are not tested.

**Relevance to your work:** This paper directly addresses the Forecasting keyword with a novel and rigorously motivated PEFT technique. The rsLoRA stabilization mechanism is technically novel (absent from prior PEFT methods), and the efficiency gains enable forecasting in constrained deployment settings. Causality is not engaged.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The rsLoRA rank-stabilization principle could be applied to causal effect estimators based on LLMs — e.g., neural IV regression or causal representation models that use pre-trained language encoders. If the rank-stabilization provides gradient stability at low ranks, it may enable more reliable fine-tuning of LLM-based causal models from small observational datasets (common in Mendelian randomization settings with limited GWAS data). Additionally, the one-for-all universality claim across forecasting horizons raises the question of whether a single causal model can similarly generalize across different intervention settings without retraining.

## 5. Hybrid Quantum-Classical Spatiotemporal Forecasting for 3D Cloud Fields
**Score:** 5/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29407v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29407v1)

**Problem:** 3D cloud field forecasting requires modeling nonlocal spatial dependencies and multiscale dynamics, but existing spatiotemporal models (convolutions, recurrence, attention) are locality-biased and fail to preserve fine volumetric cloud structures.

**Solution:** QENO introduces a topology-aware quantum enhancement block that models nonlocal couplings in latent space using quantum-inspired operations, combined with a dynamic fusion temporal unit integrating quantum measurement features with recurrent memory.

**Limitations:** The 'quantum' enhancement is implemented classically (quantum-inspired), so actual quantum advantage is not demonstrated. The topology-aware component's superiority over purely classical global attention mechanisms is not ablated in isolation.

**Relevance to your work:** This paper directly targets the Forecasting keyword with a novel architectural contribution and strong empirical evaluation on a real-world meteorological dataset. The causality angle is absent — quantum-inspired methods here are used for representational expressiveness, not causal modeling.

**Research ideas this opens up:** Quantum-inspired tensor operations capture nonlocal dependencies in a way that classical attention does not. This raises the question of whether quantum-inspired representations could also capture long-range causal dependencies in time series more faithfully than transformers — particularly for causal discovery in high-dimensional observational data. A comparison of causal graph recovery performance between classical and quantum-inspired architectures would be a novel and testable research question.
