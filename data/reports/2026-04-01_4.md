## 1. Causality-inspired Federated Learning for Dynamic Spatio-Temporal Graphs
**Score:** 7/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29384v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29384v1)

**Problem:** Federated graph learning methods for dynamic spatio-temporal graphs assume uniform feature transferability across clients, causing spurious representation entanglement and negative transfer under spatial and temporal heterogeneity.

**Solution:** SC-FSGL uses a Conditional Separation Module to disentangle invariant causal factors from spurious signals via soft interventions and client-conditioned masks, complemented by a Causal Codebook that aligns causal prototypes across clients via contrastive learning.

**Limitations:** The 'causal' disentanglement is not formally grounded in a structural causal model; the paper does not provide identifiability guarantees for the invariant factors; the method is validated on predictive metrics without verifying whether recovered features are causally interpretable.

**Relevance to your work:** Directly engages the user's 'Causality' keyword in the context of spatio-temporal forecasting. The paper's goal of separating invariant causal structure from environment-specific noise aligns closely with the user's causal representation learning work ('The Third Pillar of Causal Analysis'). The federated setting adds a new axis: clients as distinct environments for causal invariance testing.

**Research ideas this opens up:** This paper reveals a gap: federated environments are a natural setting for testing causal invariance across heterogeneous distributions, analogous to multi-environment causal discovery. Applying formal invariant causal prediction or the user's causal representation framework to formally certify what is learned as invariant—rather than treating it as an inductive bias—could substantially strengthen the theoretical foundations. Additionally, the 'Causal Codebook' is reminiscent of sparse representation methods; connecting it to the user's sparse causal effect estimation work could yield new identifiability results.

## 2. Central limit theorems for the outputs of fully convolutional neural networks with time series input
**Score:** 5/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29612v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29612v1)

**Problem:** Despite the widespread use of fully convolutional networks for time series classification and forecasting, no formal theory exists for the distributional behavior of their outputs as time series length grows.

**Solution:** The authors prove that FCN outputs with global average pooling are asymptotically Gaussian when inputs are short-range dependent linear processes, and propose a generalization with slowly varying learnable pooling coefficients.

**Limitations:** Results are restricted to FCNs with GAP, short-range dependent linear processes, and do not yet cover long-memory processes, nonlinear inputs, or architectures with recurrence or attention; practical implications for calibration remain unexplored.

**Relevance to your work:** Relevant to the user's 'Forecasting' keyword through the theoretical analysis of time series models. The Gaussianity result could inform uncertainty quantification in forecasting pipelines. Less directly connected to causality, though distributional theory for neural networks is foundational for causal inference with learned representations.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The CLT result is derived for linear processes; an open question is whether analogous results hold for outputs of causal time series models where the input process is driven by a structural causal model with non-Gaussian innovations. This could connect to the user's causal change point detection work: if model outputs are asymptotically Gaussian under the null causal structure, change points could be detected via standard CUSUM or likelihood-ratio tests.

## 3. Improving Ensemble Forecasts of Abnormally Deflecting Tropical Cyclones with Fused Atmosphere-Ocean-Terrain Data
**Score:** 5/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29200v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29200v1)

**Problem:** Deep learning TC forecasting models fail on abnormally deflecting tropical cyclones because they process single-type sequential data and lack explicit coupling between atmospheric, oceanic, and terrain physical domains.

**Solution:** The authors construct a multimodal dataset (AOT-TCs) fusing atmosphere, ocean, and terrain variables, and propose the first TC model with explicit atmosphere-ocean-terrain coupling architecture.

**Limitations:** Limited to the Northwest Pacific basin (2017–2024), which may limit generalizability; the coupling architecture is task-specific and may not transfer to other weather phenomena; no uncertainty quantification on rare deflection events.

**Relevance to your work:** Directly relevant to the 'Forecasting' keyword. The paper exposes a key open problem in meteorological forecasting: identifying which cross-domain couplings drive rare anomalous events. The user's causal change point detection work could be relevant to detecting when a TC transitions from normal to anomalous deflection.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The framework reveals an important gap: identifying the causal drivers of TC deflection anomalies rather than just improving predictive accuracy. The user's causal change point detection methods could be applied to pinpoint when and where physical causality shifts during anomalous TC tracks, turning a prediction problem into a causal discovery problem over multivariate physical fields.

## 4. One-for-All: A Lightweight Stabilized and Parameter-Efficient Pre-trained LLM for Time Series Forecasting
**Score:** 5/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29756v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29756v1)

**Problem:** Adapting pre-trained LLMs for time series analysis is hindered by prohibitive computational and memory costs, and existing PEFT methods (e.g., LoRA) lack gradient stability guarantees at low ranks.

**Solution:** rsLoRA injects Gaussian rank-stabilized low-rank adaptation matrices into positional embeddings and output layers while freezing attention weights, achieving provable gradient stability and 6.8–21x fewer trainable parameters than baselines.

**Limitations:** The gradient stability proof applies to the optimization landscape but does not guarantee better generalization across time series domains; evaluation is restricted to standard benchmark datasets without out-of-distribution testing; the theoretical analysis of rank stabilization is limited to convergence, not sample complexity.

**Relevance to your work:** Relevant to the 'Forecasting' keyword. The rank-stabilization idea is a technically novel contribution to parameter-efficient fine-tuning for time series. There is no direct connection to causality, but the work raises questions about what structure in pre-trained LLMs transfers usefully to time series prediction tasks.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The paper shows that freezing attention weights while adapting positional and output layers achieves competitive performance—implying LLM attention encodes transferable structure. An open causal question is: what invariances are the frozen weights learning, and can causal regularization during pre-training or fine-tuning enforce causally meaningful invariances across time series domains rather than relying on empirical transfer?

## 5. Meteorology-Driven GPT4AP: A Multi-Task Forecasting LLM for Atmospheric Air Pollution in Data-Scarce Settings
**Score:** 4/10  |  **Paper:** [2603.29974v1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29974v1)

**Problem:** Air pollution forecasting in data-scarce regions fails to generalize because existing models lack few-shot and zero-shot transfer capability across monitoring stations with different meteorological contexts.

**Solution:** GPT4AP applies rsLoRA (same method as the concurrent One-for-All paper by the same group) to a GPT-2 backbone, fine-tuning only positional and output modules for few-shot, zero-shot, and long-term air quality forecasting.

**Limitations:** Uses essentially the same rsLoRA methodology as the companion One-for-All paper (same authors), reducing novelty; the contribution of meteorological covariates versus the LLM backbone is not ablated; zero-shot performance still lags specialized models on long-horizon tasks.

**Relevance to your work:** Relevant to 'Forecasting' through environmental monitoring. However, this paper largely duplicates the methodological contribution of 2603.29756v1 by the same group; the domain application (air pollution) is new but the core technique is the same. No connection to causality.

**Research ideas this opens up:** The zero-shot cross-station transfer setting is an implicit causal invariance problem: which features of the time series distribution are invariant across stations versus station-specific? Casting this as a formal causal transfer problem—identifying which causal mechanisms are shared—could lead to principled feature selection for zero-shot generalization rather than relying on LLM inductive bias.
